Grammar & Conjugation

Tagalog is a verb-focus language with a rich affixation system. Master the patterns and you unlock the entire language.

💡 Key Insight

Tagalog pronouns change based on their grammatical role: subject (nominative), possessive (genitive), and object/location (dative). Unlike English, Tagalog has a singular/plural distinction for "we" — including or excluding the listener.

Personal Pronouns

PersonNominative (Subject)Genitive (Possessive)Dative (Object)
1st Singular (I/me)AkoKo / AkinSa akin
2nd Singular (You)Ikaw / KaMo / IyoSa iyo
3rd Singular (He/She/It)SiyaNiyaSa kanya
1st Plural Incl. (We — you & I)TayoNatinSa atin
1st Plural Excl. (We — not you)KamiNaminSa amin
2nd Plural (You all)KayoNinyoSa inyo
3rd Plural (They)SilaNilaSa kanila
✏️ Usage Examples

Ako ay Pilipino. — I am Filipino.
Ikaw ay maganda. — You are beautiful.
Kaibigan niya si Maria. — Maria is her friend.
Tayo ay magkakain. — We (you and I) will eat together.

💡 The Heart of Tagalog Grammar

Tagalog builds meaning through affixes — prefixes, suffixes, and infixes added to root words (salita). One root can become dozens of words. The affix also signals grammatical focus: who is doing the action, what is being acted upon, or where.

um-

Actor Focus — Intransitive / Motion verbs

Added before the first vowel of the root (infix) or as a prefix before consonants. The subject performs the action. Used for intransitive or motion verbs.

kain → kumain (to eat)
lakad → lumakad (to walk)
punta → pumunta (to go)
sulat → sumulat (to write)
tulog → matulog (to sleep)
mag-

Actor Focus — Activities & Routines

Used for deliberate activities, routines, and transitive actions where the actor is the focus. Very common in everyday speech.

luto → magluto (to cook)
trabaho → magtrabaho (to work)
aral → mag-aral (to study)
basa → magbasa (to read)
linis → maglinis (to clean)
-in

Object Focus — Suffix

When the object of the action is the grammatical focus, the verb takes -in as a suffix. The sentence emphasizes what is being done to.

kain → kainin (to eat [it])
basa → basahin (to read [it])
bigay → bigyan (to give [to])
punta → puntahan (to go to [a place])
-an

Locative / Benefactive Focus

Focuses on the location, recipient, or beneficiary of the action. Can also intensify or indicate direction.

linis → linisin (to clean [it])
sulat → sulatan (to write to)
takbo → takbuhan (to run to/from)
ma-

Adjective / Stative Prefix

Creates adjectives from nouns and describes states or qualities. Extremely productive — most Tagalog adjectives use this prefix.

ganda → maganda (beautiful)
bait → mabait (kind, good)
lakas → malakas (strong)
bilis → mabilis (fast)
hirap → mahirap (difficult, poor)
nag- / nag- + -in

Past Tense — Actor / Object Focus

The past tense of mag- verbs uses nag-. For um- verbs in the past, the infix stays but the context changes. Past tense signals completed actions.

magluto → nagluto (cooked)
kumain → kumain (ate — same form)
pumunta → pumunta (went — same form)
mag-aral → nag-aral (studied)
💡 Aspect, Not Tense

Tagalog technically uses aspect rather than strict tense — verbs show whether an action is completed, in progress, or anticipated. Context usually clarifies the time frame.

Verb Conjugation: kumain (to eat)

AspectTagalog FormExample SentenceMeaning
InfinitiveKumainGusto kong kumain ng sushi.I want to eat sushi.
Past (Completed)KumainKumain na ako.I have already eaten.
Present (Ongoing)KumakainKumakain siya ngayon.She is eating now.
Future (Contemplated)KakainKakain tayo mamaya.We will eat later.

Verb Conjugation: magluto (to cook)

AspectTagalog FormExample SentenceMeaning
InfinitiveMaglutoMarunong siyang magluto.She knows how to cook.
Past (Completed)NaglutoNagluto kami ng adobo.We cooked adobo.
Present (Ongoing)NaglulutoNagluluto ang nanay ko.My mother is cooking.
Future (Contemplated)MaglulutoMagluluto ako bukas.I will cook tomorrow.
📌 The Pattern

For um- verbs: Past = same as infinitive (with context marker na) | Present = double first syllable | Future = first syllable only

For mag- verbs: Past = change mag→nag | Present = change mag→naglalo | Future = change mag→maglalo

💡 Verb-Initial Language

Tagalog typically places the verb first, followed by the subject and then objects. However, word order is flexible — emphasis shifts the focus. The particle ang marks the subject/topic, ng marks the object, and sa marks location or direction.

Basic Particles

ParticleFunctionExampleTranslation
AngSubject/Topic markerKumain ang bata.The child ate.
NgObject marker / possessiveKumain siya ng gulay.She ate vegetables.
SaLocation / direction / dativePumunta siya sa palengke.She went to the market.
SiPersonal name subject markerKumain si Maria.Maria ate.
NiPersonal name object/possessiveLibro ni Juan.Juan's book.
KayPersonal name dativeIbinigay ko kay Pedro.I gave it to Pedro.
AyTopic inversion markerAng bata ay kumain.The child ate. (formal)

✅ Basic Sentence

Nagluto ang babae ng adobo sa kusina.

The woman cooked adobo in the kitchen.

Nagluto = cooked (verb)
ang babae = the woman (topic)
ng adobo = adobo (object)
sa kusina = in the kitchen (location)

📌 Negation

To negate, use hindi before the verb:

Hindi kumain ang bata. — The child did not eat.

Hindi ako marunong mag-Tagalog. — I don't know Tagalog.

Huwag kumain ng marami! — Don't eat too much! (command)

💡 Yes/No Questions

To form a yes/no question, simply raise your intonation at the end of a statement, or add ba after the first word or verb:

Kumain ka na ba? — Have you eaten yet?
Maganda ba siya? — Is she beautiful?
Pilipino ka ba? — Are you Filipino?

Numbers & Time

Filipinos use both native Tagalog and Spanish-derived numbers in daily life.

💡 Two Number Systems

The Philippines officially uses native Tagalog numbers, but due to Spanish colonization, Spanish-derived numbers are more commonly used in daily life — especially for money, time, and large numbers.

💡 Spanish Influence

Spain colonized the Philippines for over 300 years (1565–1898), leaving a deep mark on the language. Spanish numbers became embedded in everyday speech — you'll hear uno, dos, tres far more than native equivalents in markets, stores, and conversations about money.

⏰ Telling Time in Tagalog

Time is typically expressed using Spanish-derived numbers. Alas (from a las) precedes the hour, and ng umaga/tanghali/hapon/gabi indicates AM/PM.

English Words From Filipino

These English words came from Tagalog and other Philippine languages.

Boondocks
From bundok (mountain). Used by American soldiers in WWII.
Yo-yo
Borrowed from Filipino dialects — a traditional toy in Luzon.
Bamboo
From Malay bambu, closely related to Filipino usage.
Gung-ho
Popularized through military contact with Filipinos during WWII.
